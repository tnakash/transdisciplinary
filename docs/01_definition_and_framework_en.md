# Transdisciplinary Engineering (TE): Definition and Framework

*English version of [01_definition_and_framework.md](./01_definition_and_framework.md).*

## 1. Background: Evolution from Concurrent Engineering

Transdisciplinary Engineering (TE) is an emerging field that extends and evolves the foundational concepts of **Concurrent Engineering (CE)**, developed in the 1980s.

- ISPE, Inc. (International Society of Productivity Enhancement), the society that promoted CE, renamed its conference series to "Transdisciplinary Engineering" in 2016 in response to the growing importance of transdisciplinarity, and in 2017 the society itself was renamed **ISTE (International Society of Transdisciplinary Engineering)**.
- While CE focuses on "collaboration within and across enterprises, and the integration of the diverse elements (people, processes, organizations) involved," TE builds on this foundation while integrating knowledge from natural science (engineering) and social science, addressing broader and more complex contemporary societal challenges.

Sources: [ISTE – About](https://intsoctransde.org/about/), [TE2024 – What is TE?](https://www.te2024.org.uk/about/what-is-te), [TE2022 – Transdisciplinary Engineering](https://www.te2022.org/transdisciplinary-engineering)

## 2. Hierarchy of Disciplinarity: Multi / Inter / Transdisciplinary

Based on the general classification of interdisciplinarity research (Nicolescu, Klein, etc.), the degree of integration can be hierarchized as follows. TE is positioned at the highest level of integration within this hierarchy.

| Level | Definition | Relationship of knowledge |
|---|---|---|
| Multidisciplinary | Multiple disciplinary perspectives examine a problem in parallel, without integration | Juxtaposition |
| Interdisciplinary | Methods, theories, and tools from multiple disciplines are transferred and integrated to build unified understanding | Interaction |
| Transdisciplinary | Crosses the ontological and epistemological boundaries of disciplines, integrating academic knowledge with practical knowledge (society, industry, users) | True synthesis / co-creation |

Sources: [Nicolescu – Methodology of Transdisciplinarity](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/268353025_Methodology_of_Transdisciplinarity-Levels_of_Reality_Logic_of_the_Included_Middle_and_Complexity), Klein (comparison of interdisciplinarity/transdisciplinarity)

## 3. Convergence of Definitions in TE Research: Three Core Characteristics

The most systematic empirical treatment of "defining TE" is Lattanzio, Newnes, Parry & Nassehi (2021), *"Concepts of transdisciplinary engineering: a transdisciplinary landscape,"* *International Journal of Agile Systems and Management*, 14(2), 292-312.

Based on a survey of 34 authors at the ISTE international conference (TE2020), this study concluded that "no single definition exists; TE should instead be understood as a 'landscape' composed of three characteristic axes." These three characteristics were mentioned by 91% of participants as core concepts.

1. **Goal**: What the research aims to achieve (e.g., solving ill-defined/wicked problems, social relevance, innovation creation)
2. **Collaboration**: Who is collaborated with, and how (cross-disciplinary experts, industry-academia partnerships, co-creation with users/society)
3. **Integration**: How knowledge, methodologies, and processes are integrated (juxtaposed, or truly integrated)

Wognum, Bil, Elgh, Peruzzini, Stjepandić & Verhagen (2019), *"Transdisciplinary systems engineering: implications, challenges and research agenda,"* *IJASM*, 12(1), 58-89, similarly positions TE as "a process for solving ill-defined and socially relevant problems," and organizes the challenges across three areas: research, practice, and education.

## 4. Working Definition of TE (Adopted for This Project)

Based on the literature review above, this project adopts the following as the **working definition of Transdisciplinary Engineering**:

> Transdisciplinary Engineering (TE) is a research, practice, and education approach that builds on Concurrent Engineering while integrating knowledge and methodologies from natural science (engineering) and social science, through collaboration with diverse stakeholders across academia, industry, and society, to address ill-defined and socially relevant challenges (innovation involving products, processes, organizations, or social systems) that cannot be solved by any single discipline alone.

## 5. Structuring the Components (Framework)

Using the three core characteristics (Goal / Collaboration / Integration) as the primary axes, the sub-elements extracted from the literature are structured as follows.

```
Transdisciplinary Engineering
│
├── A. Goal (Goal characteristics)
│   ├── A1. Nature of the problem (ill-defined / wicked problem, or well-structured problem)
│   ├── A2. Social relevance (explicitness of impact on society, environment, economy)
│   └── A3. Innovation context (product, process, organization, or social environment)
│
├── B. Collaboration (Collaboration characteristics)
│   ├── B1. Breadth of cross-disciplinarity (multiple engineering sub-disciplines / engineering × social science-humanities)
│   ├── B2. Diversity of stakeholders (academia, industry, government, citizens, end users)
│   └── B3. Mode of user/practitioner participation (passive information provision / co-creation and joint design)
│
├── C. Integration (Integration characteristics)
│   ├── C1. Depth of knowledge integration (juxtaposition → cross-reference → true integration)
│   ├── C2. Methodology & tools (mixed methods, lifecycle integration, PLM/PCP integration, etc.)
│   └── C3. Process integration (early and parallel integration within the Product Creation Process)
│
└── D. Application domain and context (Wognum et al. 2019's three domains + application themes)
    ├── D1. Research: theory and framework building
    ├── D2. Practice: industrial application and case studies
    ├── D3. Education: TE talent development and curricula
    └── D4. Example application themes: Industry 4.0, social innovation, resilience,
        sustainable mobility, complex socio-technical systems
```
